The Billion Dollar Gamble... Was it Worth it?

The Series 1 Mazda RX-7, produced from 1978 to 1980 and commonly known as the "SA22C," marked a significant chapter in Mazda's automotive history. Matasaburo Maeda, the lead designer at Mazda, crafted a masterpiece that not only replaced the Savanna RX-3 in Japan but also showcased the brand's commitment to rotary engine-powered excellence. The RX-7, designated as X605 during its internal development, stood out with its minimal size, lightweight design, and a compact rotary engine strategically positioned behind the front axle for optimal weight distribution. This configuration granted the RX-7 a low center of gravity, a key factor in its sports car prowess. Beyond its engineering marvel, the RX-7 gained favor in the Japanese market by complying with government dimension regulations, sparing buyers from yearly taxes for driving a larger car. The rotary engine's displacement below 1,500 cc further appealed to Japanese consumers due to its cost-effective road tax implications. In May 1980, Mazda introduced the Leathersport Models in North America, a limited edition with distinctive features like LS badges, gold anodized wheels, and full brown leather upholstery. This special edition, available in Aurora White, Brilliant Black, and Solar Gold, added a touch of luxury to the RX-7's performance-oriented lineage. The Series 1 RX-7, with its exposed steel bumpers and unique rear license plate indentation, left an indelible mark on automotive enthusiasts, capturing the essence of design excellence and engineering innovation.

The second-generation RX-7, often referred to as "FC," exhibited a comprehensive redesign, embracing the softer sport-tourer trends of its time while enhancing handling and performance. Led by Chief Project Engineer Akio Uchiyama, Mazda's development team focused on the American market, drawing inspiration from successful sports cars like the Porsche 928. The shift from the live rear axle to Independent Rear Suspension (IRS) significantly improved the rear-end design, reducing oversteer tendencies. The FC RX-7 featured rack and pinion steering, standard disc brakes, and optional rear seats in some models. Innovations like the Dynamic Tracking Suspension System (DTSS) and Auto Adjusting Suspension (AAS) contributed to improved driving dynamics, providing passive rear steering and adaptable damping characteristics. In Japan, the limited edition Infini series, produced in various series from 1987 to 1990, stood out with its exclusive features, upgraded suspension, higher power output, and distinctive exterior elements. The Turbo II model, featuring a twin-scroll turbocharger, showcased a nuanced design to counter turbo lag, and its air-to-air intercooler contributed to increased power output. The Australian Motors Mazda introduced a unique 'Sports' model Series 4 RX-7, limiting it to 250 units, stripping away power steering, power windows, and rear wipers to reduce the car's weight and enhance its performance appeal.

The third-generation RX-7, commonly known as FD (chassis code FD3S for Japan and JM1FD for North America), debuted in October 1991, featuring a revolutionary body design. With production commencing later that month and sales launching in December in Japan, the Left-Hand-Drive export followed shortly after. The FD RX-7 was equipped with the 13B-REW engine, introducing the first-ever mass-produced sequential twin-turbocharger system exported from Japan. Designed by Yoichi Sato, with contributions from Wu-huang Chin, this model marked a departure from Japanese dimension regulations, impacting domestic sales with annual taxes for its non-compliant width. As an upper-level luxury sports car due to increased dimensions, Mazda countered by offering smaller alternatives like the Eunos Roadster and the Eunos Presso hatchback in Japan. The intricate sequential twin-turbocharging system, developed with Hitachi, brought complexity to the engine. Operating with two turbochargers, it provided a boost at different rpm ranges, offering semi-linear acceleration and a wide torque curve throughout the rev range under normal conditions. However, the system's complexity and susceptibility to failure due to high temperatures posed challenges, requiring technical drivers to adapt their driving style, especially during high-speed conditions and cornering.
