GT-R: How Nissan Created the Giantkiller

The Nissan GT-R, designated as R35, has been a pinnacle in Japanese automotive engineering since its debut in 2007, marking the revival of the GT-R brand as part of Nissan's ambitious Revival Plan. Departing from its Skyline lineage, this 2+2 sports car and grand tourer boasts a front-mid engine configuration and an all-wheel-drive system, setting it apart from its predecessors. Developed over seven years, the GT-R made its production debut in 2007 at the Tokyo Motor Show, showcasing groundbreaking features like advanced aerodynamics, the VR38DETT engine, an active suspension system, and the innovative ATTESA E-TS Pro all-wheel-drive system. Notably, it became one of the first rear-mounted independent transaxle all-wheel-drive vehicles globally, featuring launch control and a dual-clutch transmission. Constructed on the PM platform, derived from the FM platform utilized in Skyline and Nissan Z models, the GT-R combines steel, aluminum, and carbon-fiber components. Its global availability, frequent facelifts, and special editions testify to its enduring popularity, complemented by its impressive motorsports record and critical acclaim, earning accolades like the World Performance Car of The Year and Top Gear's recognition as "one of the most incredible cars ever built." Celebrating its one-year anniversary, the Nissan GT-R remains an iconic blend of performance, practicality, and affordability in the world of high-performance automobiles.

In the ambitious pursuit of rejuvenating the Nissan brand, CEO Carlos Ghosn initiated the revival of the iconic GT-R as a centerpiece of the Nissan Revival Plan in 2000. Ghosn envisioned the GT-R not just as a sports car but as a high-performance contender capable of rivaling the likes of Porsche, Lamborghini, and Ferrari, all while maintaining an affordable price point. This endeavor brought together a team of top engineers and designers, under the leadership of Chief Engineer Kazutoshi Mizuno, known as Mr. GT-R. Initially hesitant due to platform constraints, Mizuno eventually gained autonomy and spearheaded the development on the innovative Premium Midship (PM) platform. Concurrently, a specialized team, curated by Ghosn, meticulously developed the drivetrain and chassis, pushing the GT-R's performance boundaries during extensive testing at the Nürburgring and Sendai Hi-Land Raceway. The engineering marvel continued with the creation of the VR38DETT 3.8L twin-turbocharged V6 engine, a departure from the initially planned straight-6 engine. Simultaneously, a dedicated team tackled the complex challenge of aerodynamics, aiming for a delicate balance between downforce and minimal drag. Over six years of development, involving numerous wind tunnel tests and refinements, the engineers achieved a visually appealing exterior with a drag coefficient of Cd=0.27 combined with front and rear downforce. Finally, after a dedicated year of testing, Nissan proudly unveiled the production version at the 2007 Tokyo Motor Show, marking the culmination of years of innovation, determination, and collaborative effort. Ghosn's vision materialized, dispelling rumors and confirming the global release of the GT-R as a Nissan model, a testament to its potential profitability and enduring impact on the automotive world.

The production version of the GT-R made a dramatic entrance at the 2007 Tokyo Motor Show, solidifying its reputation as a groundbreaking supercar. The unveiling followed an exhilarating on-screen showcase of the GT-R's capabilities, featuring a remarkable 7:38.54-minute lap time in challenging, damp conditions around the Nürburgring Nordschleife. This impressive performance outshone the benchmark Porsche 911 Turbo, which held a lap time of 7:40 minutes, leading Nissan to proudly claim the record for the fastest lap time for a mass-produced car. Positioned as "A supercar, for Anyone, Anywhere, Anytime," the GT-R's unveiling generated significant anticipation and excitement. In November 2007, just before its official launch, Nissan revealed an overwhelming demand for the GT-R, with more than 3,000 buyers pre-ordering the car in Japan. This fervor reflected the global enthusiasm for this high-performance marvel. To maintain exclusivity, Nissan aimed to sell approximately 200 units per month. Deliveries commenced on December 6, 2007, in Japan, followed by North America on July 7, 2008, and Europe in March 2009. Notably, the first production GT-R was proudly acquired by Nissan's CEO at the time, Carlos Ghosn, underscoring the significance of the GT-R in Nissan's leadership. The staggered regional releases were influenced by the strategic need for Nissan to establish GT-R performance centers for servicing, reflecting the brand's commitment to providing exceptional customer experiences worldwide.
