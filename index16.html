NSX: How Honda Broke Supercars

The Honda NSX, known as the Acura NSX in North America, is a remarkable two-seater, mid-engined coupe sports car crafted by Honda. Originating in 1984 with the HP-X (Honda Pininfarina eXperimental) concept, featuring a mid-engine 3.0 L V6 engine and rear-wheel drive, the NSX project was born with the ambitious goal of surpassing the performance of Ferrari's V8 engine range while maintaining reliability and a more accessible price point. The concept evolved, adopting the name NS-X, representing "New," "Sportscar," and "eXperimental." When launched as the NSX, it left an indelible mark on the automotive world. Notably, Gordon Murray, the visionary behind the McLaren F1 supercar, drew inspiration from the NSX after test-driving numerous high-performance vehicles. Impressed by the NSX's chassis performance, Murray considered it "monumental" for sportscar design, even though he advocated for a more potent engine, a suggestion Honda declined. Despite this, Murray's admiration for the NSX endured, leading him to personally own and drive one for 75,000 kilometers, declaring the NSX "dear to his heart."

Designed under the leadership of Chief Designer Masahito Nakano and Executive Chief Engineer Shigeru Uehara, the NSX stands as an automotive marvel, incorporating advanced aerodynamics and drawing inspiration from the cockpit of an F-16 fighter jet. Notably, the late Formula One World Champion Ayrton Senna contributed invaluable insights during the final stages of development. This groundbreaking vehicle marked a historic milestone as the world's first mass-produced car with an all-aluminium body. Its powerhouse featured an all-aluminium 3.0 L V6 engine equipped with Honda's innovative VTEC (Variable Valve Timing and Lift Electronic Control) system, complemented by a 5-speed manual transmission or the SportShift 4-speed automatic transmission introduced in 1994. Premiering at the 1989 Chicago Auto Show and produced in a purpose-built factory in Japan, the NSX became available for purchase in 1990, initially as a coup√© and later, from 1995, as a targa top. The first-generation NSX underwent significant transformations, including a performance upgrade in 1997 with a larger 3.2 L V6 engine and a 2002 facelift that bid farewell to the iconic pop-up headlights. North American models bore the Acura NSX moniker, and the first-generation NSX concluded its illustrious run in 2005, distinguishing between NA1 models with the 3.0 L C30A engine and NA2 models featuring the 3.2 L C32B engine.

The NSX GT1 and GT2 Le Mans race cars, originating from factory NSX-R shells, underwent a radical transformation at TC Prototypes in England. Eleven shells were meticulously reworked, incorporating a custom dry carbon fiber tub and additional pieces to enhance chassis stiffness. The suspension received a bespoke setup with race dampers, aluminum control arms, and center-nut style wheel hubs, while wide body fenders accommodated race tires, enhancing downforce. The C30A engine underwent extensive revisions, featuring a dry sump oiling system, larger cams, and inconel exhaust headers. GT2 variants boasted a massive carbon fiber intake plenum with six individual throttle bodies, while GT1 engines adopted a turbocharger with a custom dual-plenum aluminum manifold. Utilizing bespoke engine management, the GT2 engines produced nearly 400 bhp, a substantial increase from the production version, and the turbocharged GT1 engines exceeded 600 bhp, marking them as Honda's most powerful NSX engines. Both engines were coupled with a Hewland 6-speed sequential transmission. The NSX made noteworthy appearances at the 24 Hours of Le Mans in 1994, 1995, and 1996. In 1994, three NSXs entered the GT2 class, finishing 14th, 16th, and 18th. The 1995 edition saw factory and Team Kunimitsu entries, with a GT2 NSX securing 8th place. The 1996 race featured the return of Team Kunimitsu, finishing 16th overall and third in the GT2 class after completing 305 laps.
