How This Overpowered Porsche Killed Its Owners

In the late 1960s, Porsche delved into the realm of turbocharging technology, initially experimenting on their race cars. By 1972, the German automaker embarked on the development of a turbocharged iteration of their iconic 911 model. Originally conceived to meet homologation regulations, the plan was to market it as a street-legal race vehicle, akin to the 1973 Carrera 2.7 RS. However, evolving regulations by the FIA's Appendix "J” prompted a shift in strategy. With the 1975 and 1976 rule changes mandating that cars for Group 4 and Group 5 had to be production cars available for individual purchase, Porsche adapted. To meet these requirements, the 911 Turbo Carrera RSR 2.1 evolved into the production-ready 911 Turbo, entering production in 1975. By the end of the year, 400 units were completed, securing FIA homologation for Group 4 on December 6, 1975. The 1,000th 911 Turbo rolled off the assembly line on May 5, 1976, marking a significant milestone for Porsche's foray into turbocharged excellence.

Ferry Porsche presented the inaugural Turbo model, 911 Turbo No. 1, as a special gift to his sister Louise Piëch for her 70th birthday. Despite its commemorative status, this unique model showcased a blend of features, including the distinctive large rear spoiler characteristic of later production models, yet retaining the narrower bodywork of the Carrera. The rear still bore the Carrera lettering, an artifact from its earlier iteration. Notably, the 911 Turbo No. 1 boasted a turbocharged 2.7-liter boxer engine, generating 177 kW (240 hp), a precursor to the later 3.0-liter boxer engine. The evolution of turbo-technology continued under the guidance of Ernst Fuhrmann, who adapted it from the 917/30 CAN-AM car to create the Porsche 930. Equipped with a single KK&K turbocharger, the 3.0-liter flat-six engine delivered an impressive 260 PS (191 kW; 256 hp) at 5,500 rpm and 329 N⋅m (243 lb⋅ft) of torque at 4,000 rpm, a substantial enhancement over the standard Carrera. The platform underwent comprehensive modifications, including a revised suspension, larger brakes, and a stronger gearbox to harness the increased power. While some enthusiasts criticized Porsche's decision to employ a four-speed transmission, the "whale tail" rear spoiler, wider rear wheels, and flared wheel arches were instrumental in enhancing stability, aerodynamics, and overall performance.

Porsche unveiled the Turbo at the Paris Auto Show in October 1974, later debuting it for sale in the spring of 1975, with U.S. exports commencing in 1976. Known as "Turbo" (initially "Turbo Carrera" in the U.S.), this model, however, earned a notorious reputation as "the Widowmaker" due to its demanding nature and unique handling characteristics. Characterized by a short wheelbase and a rear-engine layout, the 930 was susceptible to oversteer and turbo-lag, causing accidents and earning its ominous nickname. Porsche responded with significant changes in 1978, enlarging the engine displacement to 3,299 cc, adding an air-to-air intercooler, and reconfiguring the iconic 'whale tail' spoiler. While power output increased to 300 PS, the changes also altered the car's weight distribution and handling. Withdrawn from the Japanese and U.S. markets in 1980 due to emissions regulations, the 930 remained available in Canada, and in 1983, a performance option with 330 PS became available in Europe. Porsche re-entered the U.S. market with the 930 in 1986, featuring emission-controlled engines, targa, and cabriolet variants. The 930 concluded its legacy in 1989 as the G-Series platform made way for the 964, with the 964 Turbo succeeding it in 1991, marking the evolution of the iconic Porsche 911 Turbo lineage.
