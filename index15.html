GT-R: How Japan Created an Unstoppable Monster

The Nissan Skyline GT-R, known for its iconic presence in the realm of Japanese sports cars, traces its origins back to the late 1960s with the production of the KPGC10 model, marking the inception of the "Skyline GT-R" series. These initial vehicles, manufactured between 1969 and 1972, gained acclaim for their prowess in Japanese touring car racing. Following a brief hiatus, the GT-R name was resurrected in 1989 with the introduction of the BNR32 ("R32") Skyline GT-R. The R32 quickly made a formidable impact, dominating the Japanese Touring Car Championship for four consecutive years. Its triumphs extended to the Australian Touring Car Championship, where drivers like Jim Richards and Mark Skaife secured championships in 1991 and 1992, respectively. The R32 GT-R's technological advancements and outstanding performance earned it the moniker "Godzilla," a title bestowed by the Australian motoring publication Wheels in July 1989. This nickname has since become synonymous with the Skyline GT-R's legendary status in the world of high-performance automobiles.

After the discontinuation of the Skyline GT-R in 1973, Nissan orchestrated a remarkable revival of the GT-R nameplate in 1989, spurred by their participation in Group A Racing with the Skyline GTS-R. Seeking a more competitive contender, Nissan introduced the E-BNR32 chassis, commonly referred to as the R32, specifically engineered to assert dominance in the Group A class racing circuit. Initially testing a twin-turbocharged 2.4 L version of the RB20 engine at Nissan Kohki, the powertrain engineering and manufacturing facility, the output reached 233 kW (317 PS; 312 hp), employing a rear-wheel-drive drivetrain. To adhere to Group A regulations and enhance traction, Nissan implemented an all-wheel-drive system named the ATTESA E-TS. However, the newfound traction came at the cost of additional weight, placing the GT-R at a disadvantage. To address this, Nissan increased the displacement to 2,600 cc, situating the car in the 4,500 cc class, accompanied by an adjusted weight and the allowance for 11-inch-wide tires. This strategic move resulted in a powerful machine boasting an impressive 441 kW (600 PS; 592 hp). The evolution continued with REINIK, Nissan Kohi's Racing & Rally Engineering Division, producing Group A racing engines ranging from 373 to 485 kW (507–659 PS; 500–650 hp), adapting to various track conditions and solidifying the GT-R's formidable presence in motorsport.

The GT-R's storied legacy of racetrack supremacy can be traced back to its early triumphs from 1968 to 1972, amassing an impressive 50 victories, with a remarkable 49 consecutive wins on Japanese race circuits. This era of dominance, however, faced an abrupt hiatus with Nissan's withdrawal from racing following the introduction of the KPGC110 due to the 1973 oil crisis. The GT-R's resurgence in the late '80s, notably with the R32 model, ushered in a new era of glory. Affectionately dubbed "Godzilla" for its monstrous track performance and Japanese origin, the R32 GT-R asserted its dominance by clinching victory in all 29 races it entered in the Japanese Touring Car Championship, securing the series title every year from 1989 to 1993. The GT-R's triumphs extended beyond Japan, resonating at iconic races such as the Mount Panorama circuit in Bathurst, where it claimed back-to-back victories in 1991 and 1992 despite weight penalties and setbacks. The GT-R's success reverberated through motorsport history, contributing to the evolution of racing categories like Super GT and leaving an enduring legacy that continues with its successor, the Nissan GT-R, which dominated the 2008 Super GT season.
