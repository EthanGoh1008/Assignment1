How a Bunch of Rebels Built a Killer Supercar

Conceived in late 1988 at Chrysler's Advanced Design Studios, the Dodge Viper's inception traced back to a visionary idea. Chrysler president Bob Lutz, in February of the following year, proposed the production of a modern Cobra to Tom Gale at Chrysler Design Center. A clay model materialized a few months later and, produced in sheet metal by Metalcrafters, debuted as a concept at the North American International Auto Show in 1989. The overwhelming public enthusiasm prompted chief engineer Roy Sjoberg to embark on its development as a standard production vehicle. However, Chrysler chairman Lee Iacocca initially hesitated, delaying approval for the required $70 million investment due to concerns about financial return. Undeterred, Sjoberg assembled "Team Viper" in March 1989, collaborating with Lamborghini for a prototype aluminum block. By May 1990, official approval was granted, and in November 1991, reviewers got their hands on the Viper, with retail shipments commencing in January 1992. Priced at $52,000, the powerful two-seater not only captured enthusiasts' hearts but also revitalized the Dodge brand, overshadowing previous setbacks and earning admiration from both automotive press and enthusiasts alike.

The Dodge Viper, known for its raw and purist approach to performance, eschewed modern driver aids like traction control and anti-lock brakes, offering a driving experience devoid of electronic interventions. The absence of exterior-mounted door handles and key locks meant unconventional entry – achieved by unzipping a vinyl window to reach the interior door release handle, especially when the canvas or hard top was in place. In its early iterations, the Viper lacked air conditioning and airbags for weight reduction purposes, and its roof was made from canvas, with vinyl windows operated by zippers. Despite these Spartan choices, the Viper accommodated daily driving with manually-adjustable seats featuring lumbar support, an AM/FM cassette stereo player, a clock, and carpeting. Later models introduced the option for a lightweight fiberglass hard top as an alternative to the standard canvas soft top, providing further versatility. The collaboration with Lamborghini, then owned by Chrysler Corporation, played a pivotal role in crafting the aluminum-alloy V10 engine, drawing inspiration from Chrysler's LA engine. Chief power engineer Dick Winkles, a significant contributor to the engine project, even spent time in Italy for its development, highlighting the international influence on the Viper's iconic powertrain.

The heart of the Dodge Viper's performance lies in its formidable engine, boasting a maximum power output of 400 hp at 4,600 rpm and a robust torque of 465 lb⋅ft at 3,600 rpm, all while weighing 323 kg. This powerhouse, coupled with long gearing, contributes to the Viper's fuel economy, rated at 12 mpg during city driving and an impressive 20 mpg on the highway by the United States Environmental Protection Agency. The Viper's agility is further accentuated by its curb weight of 1,490 kg, featuring a tubular steel frame and resin-transfer molding fiberglass panels. Accelerating from 0–60 mph in 4.6 seconds and reaching a maximum speed of 165 mph, the Viper's prowess on the road is underscored by its quarter-mile time of 12.6 seconds at a speed of 113.8 mph. With large tires providing an average lateral g-force of 0.96 in corners, the Viper stood among the best performance cars of its era, although its formidable capabilities made it a demanding drive for the unskilled.
