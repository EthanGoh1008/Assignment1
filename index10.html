How Japan's Greatest Supercar Flourished and Failed

The Lexus LFA, a masterpiece born from meticulous development, stands as a testament to Lexus's pursuit of automotive excellence. Emerging as the second model in the F marque line, it marked a significant milestone as the inaugural standalone Lexus F model, captivating enthusiasts worldwide. The journey began in the early 2000s with the codenamed TXS, evolving through three captivating concept versions showcased at the North American International Auto Show. The LF-A series, unveiling its prowess progressively from 2005 to 2008, ultimately materialized into the production model, trademarked as LFA, showcased at the Tokyo Motor Show in October 2009. The "A" in LFA signifies the "Apex" of the LF line, as per Lexus. Guided by Akio Toyoda's vision, the CEO of Toyota Motor Corporation, the LFA aimed to be a global icon for the Lexus brand, forging an emotional connection between the ultimate Lexus and its owner. Under the hood, the LFA featured a 560 PS V10 engine, a result of collaboration with Yamaha, housed within a body crafted from carbon fibre-reinforced polymer (CFRP), constituting 65 percent of the car's body composition. Production commenced in late 2010, with a circuit-tuned variant adding to its allure in 2012. Despite becoming one of the most expensive Japanese road cars ever built, the LFA's production concluded in December 2012 after delivering 500 cars, with the Nürburgring Package model as the crowning achievement. Alas, in February 2016, Lexus' European boss confirmed that no new LFA replacement would grace the roads in the foreseeable future, solidifying the LFA's legacy as an extraordinary chapter in automotive history.

The developmental journey of the Lexus LFA spans over several years, encapsulating a meticulous process that unfolded in distinct phases. In the initial phase from 2000 to 2004, the LF-A sports car embarked on its development under the codename TXS, positioning itself as a showcase for Toyota Motor Corporation and the Lexus marque. Prototypes emerged by June 2003, with rigorous testing at the renowned Nürburgring track in Germany from October 2004. Transitioning to 2005–2006, the first LF-A concept emerged at the Detroit Auto Show, evolving from a design study to a potential production model due to positive public reception. The subsequent period of 2007–2008 witnessed a shift to a carbon-fibre tub, drawing resources from Toyota's Formula One team. A restyled LF-A concept debuted alongside the IS F sports sedan, signaling a closer alignment with the forthcoming F marque series. The development continued, with the introduction of a roadster version in January 2008 and exploration of hybrid options. The second LF-A concepts in 2008 showcased refined exterior designs, inspired by the 1965 2000GT sports coupe, while development continued to enhance aerodynamics and speed. By 2009, Toyota's CEO, Akio Toyoda, confirmed LF-A production, culminating in the unveiling of the Lexus LFA at the Tokyo Motor Show. Limited to 500 production copies, the LFA not only marked the 20th anniversary of Lexus but also served as a technological testbed for carbon-fibre mass production and performance vehicle development, solidifying its position as a groundbreaking icon in the automotive realm.

The Lexus LFA, a polarizing figure in the automotive world, has garnered diverse opinions from critics and enthusiasts alike. Car and Driver, in their evaluation, deemed the sports car "hugely expensive, hugely competent," suggesting that it presented a compelling value proposition compared to pricier alternatives with similar technology and exclusivity. However, Motor Trend, while praising the LFA's driving dynamics and engine note in track tests, placed it ninth out of eleven cars in their 2011 Best Driver's Car competition. The criticism centered around a "nervous, twitchy chassis" that eroded driver confidence, coupled with concerns about transmission, ergonomics, and ride quality. Autocar applauded the LFA's engine and handling on smooth surfaces but raised concerns about its performance on regular roads, describing it as "an occasional car, even by supercar standards." In contrast, GTspirit.com offered a positive review after testing the LFA in Monaco, highlighting its ease of driving and superb balance. The sentiment was echoed by various reviewers who placed the Lexus LFA among the best cars they've ever driven. Chris Harris of Evo UK lauded it as the "best car" around the Nurburgring, while Autoweek Netherlands' Sandor Van Ees ranked it among the top 5 best cars he has driven. European journalist Thomas Bangma described the LFA as an "absolute gem," asserting it as the best car he had ever driven. Car Magazine's Ben Barry dubbed the LFA Nurburgring edition as "the Bugatti Veyron of Japan," encapsulating the divergent perspectives surrounding this iconic sports car.
