This Car Won a Race Every Single Day... For YEARS

The first BMW M3, born out of the E30 3 Series, emerged as a homologation special aimed at satisfying the stringent requirements of the Deutsche Tourenwagen Meisterschaft and Group A Touring rules, mandating the production of 5,000 cars. Unveiled at the 1985 Frankfurt Motor Show, its production spanned from March 1986 to June 1991. Primarily available as a coupé, limited convertible volumes were also crafted. Beyond its distinctive body style, the E30 M3 underwent significant aerodynamic enhancements, including a flattened rear window, a raised tailgate made of lightweight glass-reinforced plastic, and alterations to the C-pillar and bootlid. These modifications not only improved aerodynamics but also resulted in a lower drag coefficient of Cd=0.33. Sharing only a few exterior panels with the standard 3 Series, such as the bonnet, roof panel, and sunroof, the M3 stood as a unique and performance-focused iteration. Notably, its brake components, transmission, and limited-slip differential distinguished it from its counterparts, contributing to its accolades as one of the top sports cars of the 1980s and earning a spot among the "5 greatest drivers cars of all time" by Automobile Magazine in 2007.

The M3 E30 showcased its prowess not only on the roads but also on the racetracks, achieving remarkable success in various motorsport disciplines, particularly touring car racing. The 1988 M3, boasting a naturally aspirated 2.3 L engine, flexed its muscles with an impressive 221 kW (300 PS; 296 hp) in full race trim. Its dominance extended to endurance events, clinching victory at the 24 Hours Nürburgring five times and the Spa 24 Hours four times, triumphs made even more remarkable as it often faced competitors with larger or turbocharged engines. Adapting to ever-changing homologation rules, BMW produced limited volumes of homologation specials like the Evo 1, Evo 2, and Sport Evolution, enhancing the M3's capabilities with weight reduction, improved aerodynamics, and increased power output. The introduction of the 2.5 L evolution engine in 1990 elevated power to around 279 kW (379 PS; 374 hp). The M3's versatility extended to rallying, where Prodrive-prepared examples contested national championships and selected WRC rounds, showcasing their prowess on asphalt, exemplified by Bernard Béguin's victory on the Tour de Corse in 1987.

The BMW M3 (E30) stands as an unparalleled collector's gem, transcending its historical significance to become a definitive milestone for BMW M. With over 1,400 race victories and a plethora of championship titles across diverse series, including world championships, the M3 E30 retains its status as the most triumphant touring car in the world. A natural-born winner, the E30 swiftly earned its laurels with more than 1,400 race wins, securing championship titles in various series, and establishing itself as a dominant force on the racing circuit. In its inaugural year, Eric van de Poele piloted the M3 to a DTM title, embodying the mantra "From zero to hero." Notably, Wilfried Vogt replicated this success in 1987, clinching the European championship in the first year of the BMW M model. Concurrently, the touring car world championship witnessed an unstoppable duo with Roberto Ravaglia behind the wheel of his M3, resulting in a resounding championship victory in the same remarkable year. The BMW M3 E30's legacy as a symbol of triumph endures, marking it as an unparalleled and iconic presence in the world of motorsport.
